Nitrogen

Nitrogen occupies 80% by volume of the atmosphere.

Example: Given 1 dm3 of air, and we know that nitrogen occupies 80% of air

then 80% of 1 dm3 = 80/100 X 1 = 0.8 dm3

In other words, there is 0.8 dm3 by volume of nitrogen in 1 dm3 of air.

Nitrogen is found in combination with other elements such as NH3 (ammonia), N2H4 (hydrazine)

Nitrogen can be prepared in the laboratory:

  • From atmospheric air

    Atmospheric air is a mixture of nitrogen gas, oxygen gas, carbon dioxide and a small amount of noble gas. In order to get nitrogen gas, the air is first passed over heated copper in a combustion tube to remove oxygen gas:

    Cu(s) + O2(g) -> 2CuO(s) (Oxygen removed)

    The leftover (containing nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and the rare gases) is then passed through aqueous sodium hydroxide, NaOH in order to remove carbon dioxide.

    NaOH(aq) + CO2(g) -> Na2CO3(aq) + H2O(l) (Carbon dioxide removed)

    Now we are left with nitrogen gas contaminated with a little amount of noble gas. Therefore, we say that the nitrogen gas obtained in this way (from the atmosphere), is not pure (impure), as it is contaminated with a small amount of noble gas.

  • From ammonium dioxonitrate(III) (NH4NO2)

    • Pure nitrogen can be obtained from the decomposition of ammonium dioxonitrate(III) (NH4NO2)

    Due to the fact that NH4NO2 might explode if heated directly, it is first produced by the reaction of sodium nitrite, NaNO2 with NH4Cl to give NH4NO2

    NaNO2(aq) + NH4Cl(aq) -> NH4NO2(aq) + NaCl(aq)

    Then the NH4NO2(aq) decomposes to give N2(nitrogen gas) and water

    NH4NO2(aq) -> N2(g) + H2O(l)

  • From ammonia gas (NH3)

    Pure nitrogen can also be gotten from the reaction of NH3, ammonia gas with copper oxide, CuO

    CuO(s) + NH3(g) -> 3Cu(s) + H2O(l) + N2(g)

Important physical properties of Nitrogen gas includes

  • Pure nitrogen is colorless and odorless.
  • It is insoluble in water.
  • It is lighter than air.

Chemical properties of Nitrogen

  • Nitrogen gas is unreactive(does not react with other elements) at ordinary temperature and pressure. Nitrogen is an inert gas which does not support combustion. It does not burn and it doesn't support burning: it extinguishes a lighted splint.
  • Nitrogen gas is neither acidic or basic when dissolved in water therefore it has no action on litmus paper. In other words, nitrogen gas is neutral.
  • Although Nitrogen is unreactive, it reacts with some metals and nonmetals.
    • It reacts with magnesium(a metal) to produce magnesium nitride, Mg3N2, which dissolves in water to give ammonia, NH3
      Reaction 1 : 3Mg(s) + N2(g) -> Mg3N2(s) (white)
      Reaction 2 : Mg3N2(s) + 6H2O(l) -> 3Mg(OH)2(aq) + 2NH3(g)
    • Nitrogen react with hydrogen(non-metal) in the haber process to give ammonia, NH3(g). Finely divided iron is used as a catalyst.
      N2(g) + 3H2(g) -> 2NH3(g)
    • Nitrogen also react with oxygen(non-metal) at high temperature and pressure, to produce nitrogen(II) oxide(NO), an unstable colorless and odorless gas.
      N2(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO(g)

Industrial production of Nitrogen

Nitrogen is produced commercially from liquefied air(a mixture of nitrogen and oxygen), by fractional distillation.

During the process, nitrogen boils off first and is collected, since it has a lower boiling point(77K) than oxygen.

Uses of Nitrogen

  • Nitrogen is used in the production of ammonia by Haber process.
  • Liquid nitrogen is used as a refrigerant.
  • Since nitrogen does not support combustion, it is used for the prevention of fire.

Ammonia

Ammonia is a covalent molecule in which an atom of nitrogen shares a pair of electrons, separately, with three hydrogen atoms. NH3

The molecule is polar, due to the large difference in the electronegativities of nitrogen and hydrogen. Ammonia is a gas normally.

Laboratory preparation of ammonia

Ammonia is majorly prepared in the laboratory by the reaction of an alkali(a soluble base) with any ammonium salt
OH- + NH4+ -> H2O(l) + NH3(g)

A typical example of the reaction above is the reaction of slaked lime(calcium hydroxide) and ammonium chloride
Ca(OH)2 + NH4Cl -> CaCl2(aq) + 2H2O(g) + NH3(g)

Ammonia can be only be dried with quicklime(calcium oxide) and silica gel.

Ammonia is collected by upward delivery(i.e downward displacement of air), since it is lighter than air.

Common drying agents such as H2SO4, fused calcium chloride, and phosphorus(V) oxide cannot be used to dry ammonia.

Physical properties of ammonia gas

  • Pure ammonia is a colorless gas with a choking smell.
  • It is lighter than air; hence it is collected by upward delivery.
  • It is highly soluble in water. Therefore it is used for the demonstration of the fountain experiment.
  • Ammonia gas is readily liquefied.

Chemical properties of Ammonia

  • Ammonia gas react with water to form an unstable ammonium hydroxide solution:
    NH3(g) + H2O(l) -> NH4OH(aq)
    The solution is weakly alkaline. A solution of ammonia gas in water is called aqueous ammonia, NH3(aq).

  • Ammonia gas turns red litmus paper blue. It is the only alkaline gas.
  • Ammonia gas neutralizes acids, being alkaline. For instance, it reacts with sulphuric acid, under two conditions:
    • When the acid used is more than the ammonia used, an acid salt is formed.
      NH3(g) + H2SO4(aq) -> NH4HSO4(s)
    • When the ammonia used is more than the acid used, a normal salt is formed:
      2NH3(g) + H2SO4(aq) -> (NH4)2SO4(aq)
      These reaction explain why concentrated sulfuric acid cannot be used to dry ammonia gas.
  • Ammonia gas burns in a supply of pure oxygen gas to produce nitrogen and water vapor.
    4NH3(g) + 3O2(g) -> 6H2O(g) + 2N2(g)
  • Ammonia gas acts as a reducing agent:
    • With copper(II) oxide: When ammonia gas is passed over heated copper(II) oxide, ammonia is oxidized to nitrogen, while black copper(II) oxide is reduced to a reddish-brown copper metal.
      3CuO(s) + 2NH3(g) -> 3Cu(s) + 3H2O(l) + 2N2(g)
    • With Chlorine gas: When dry ammonia gas is passed into a gas jar of excess of chlorine(greenish-yellow gas), a colorless mixture of gases is obtained; ammonia is oxidized to nitrogen, while chlorine is reducred to hydrogen chloride.
      2NH3(g) + 3Cl2(g) -> N2(g) + 6HCl(g)
      If ammonia is in excess, the hydrogen chloride produced combines with more ammonia gas to form a dense white fume of ammonium chloride.
      2NH3(g) + HCl(g) -> NH4Cl(s)

Industrial Preparation of Ammonia: The Haber Process

Ammonia is manufactured in large scale industrially from its constituents element(nitrogen and ammonia) by the haber process. The process was discovered by a German chemist, Fritz Haber.

  • The raw materials used are: nitrogen(obtained from liquefied air), and hydrogen(obtained from water gas or natural gas)
  • Conditions required for reaction includes:
    • pressure of about 200-250 atm
    • temperature at about 450°C
    • finely divided iron is used as catalyst(to speed up the reaction)
    N2(g) + 3H2(g) -> 2NH3(g)

During the process, nitrogen boils off first and is collected, since it has a lower boiling point(77K) than oxygen.

Uses of Nitrogen

Nitrogen is used in the production of ammonia by Haber process.

Liquid nitrogen is used as a refrigerant.

Since nitrogen does not support combustion, it is used for the prevention of fire.

It is sometimes called ammonium hydroxide, since the solution ionizes slightly, to produce OH-
